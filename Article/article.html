The history of Typography

And how it changed the world around us

by Owen Dunnigan

Typography is now something everybody does.

The art of fonts and arranging letters to make language readable is called typography. Not necessarily designing fonts, but applying fonts and choosing between a font with serif or no serifs, or whether to use a lower case or an uppercase letter to portrait a feeling or emotion. David Jury, who works at Colchester Institute in England as the head of graphic design, says "typography is now something everybody does.”(Wikipedia Contributors. “Typography”)

Typography is derived from the greek words τύπος (typos) "form" or "impression" and γράφειν (graphein) "to write". You can trace its origins to ancient times where they used stamps or dies to imprint symbols and words onto wax and various forms of materials to create currency.

In the eleventh century, the Song Dynasty in China created the first system of which movable type was possible. Their system was manufactured from ceramic tiles and was used in clay type printing until the Qing dynasty. In approximately 1490, Hua Sui introduced bronze type printing, but the movement of movable-type machines did not spread beyond East and Central Asia.

1493 was the year lead-based alloy showed up for moveable type systems. It is often acknowledged that Johannes Gutenberg was the creator of this method, still used to this day. He developed a method that used cheap letter punches with a specialized technique for casting that kicked off the start of the Printing Revolution.

In the 18th century, Joan Michaël Fleischman started producing some of the first, distinct, roman types, and was held with much respect by his contemporaries. HIs italics were based on lettering that is known as the copperplate hand. He started to record typographic history in a two volume 'book' called the Manuel Typographique, in which he introduced the first standard measurement in typography known as the point.

When the Modern Romans arrived, they "Completed trends begun by the Fell types, Fleischman, Fournier and Baskerville"(Wikipedia Contributors. “History”). This is the style of font most commonly seen today, however, "In Britain and the United States, Modern Romans...took a somewhat more rounded, less geometrical form". This can be seen in letters like the capital C, where Anglo-American faces have only one serif at the top, but European designs have two (Wikipedia Contributors. “History”).

With technology rapidly advancing, the creation of typefaces greatly changed. Almost anyone could create a font using computer introduced by IBM and with the advancement of personal computers by macintosh, you could create typefaces faster, and for more specific functions(Wikipedia Contributors. “Typography”).
